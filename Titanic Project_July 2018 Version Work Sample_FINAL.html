Question to Answer:¶

The focus of this analysis is to identify the most important factors leading to survival during the Titanic disaster. Thus the basic question to answer is:

What factors and what specific values for those specific factors maximized the odds of survival?

Key Findings¶

The analysis below shows that 'Sex', 'Pclass' (and other variables related to wealth) are the dominant factors that determined survival. In the case of 'Sex' and 'Pclass', both factors appeared to have a substantial impact as the tables and graphics below show. In addition to these, very young age also appears to be a slight factor that improved the odds of survival.

The factors that lead to enhanced rates of survival seem plausible, rather than just being spurious features of the data, in that they seem to reflect the social conventions and economic dimensions of the period. However, since we have limited information on how this information was collected and do not know if the collection might be biased in some way, it is prudent to not draw strong conclusions about the factors that led to survival. This is particularly so in the case of the variable 'Age' since in that case there were a lot of missing values and the link or trend between age and survival is very weak as noted below. The Chi Square test done below also comes with limitations in that the data does not easily avail itself to statistical inference techniques.

However, even with these limitations, the analysis, the Chi Square tests, and the visualizations below provide key insights that can be valuable to understand what shaped outcomes.

Approach and Assumptions:¶

The data on the 891 passengers of the Titanic ship is being treated as data on a specific population rather than as a sample of a larger population. This is because the sample of 891 is simply too large to take without replacement from a population of the original 2224 (10% is considered a maximum) for many statistical inference techniques to be used meaningfully. Although, one might view this Titanic data as a sample of a larger fictional population of passengers who are faced with a similar disaster, such an approach seems contrived.

Similarly, since there is limited information on whether the observations on each of the passengers is independent from other passengers or whether this data conforms to sampling methods that minimize sampling bias, statistical inference is unlikely to provide particularly insightful knowledge.

To that effect, the goal of this analysis is to largely investigate the data from various angles, and gain insight and intuition on the factors that led to survival rather than to conduct many statistical tests. However, two Chi Square tests of independence will be conducted below for illustrative purposes to gain insight on the relationship between 'Sex' and survival on the one hand and 'Pclass' (ticket class) and survival. As the analysis below will make clear, the variable 'Sex' and others indicative of wealth such as 'Pclass', 'Fare', probably played a significant role in shaping the odds of survival and thus the analysis below focuses on those factors.

In addition, there is a brief exploration of the 'Age' variable, with an aim towards gaining insight into how it might have shaped the odds of survival.

Dealing with Missing Values of Age¶

With respect to the variable 'Age' 177 values are missing. The lack of data on such a large number of passengers means that it is best to simply not remove the passengers with the missing values.

A very brief comparison (albeit not one with statistical methods or tests) which will be shown below suggests that the population with missing values for Age is different in some respects than the population for which Age is available. Thus removing the passengers with missing values might make the data set and resulting analysis less nuanced.

Thus these passengers with missing values for age will be kept in the data, but the median age of the rest of the population (28.0) will be placed as a value. This is done by creating a new variable called 'AgeNoMissValues' in which missing values will have the age of 28.0.

Chi Square Tests of Independence¶

In the cell below is code that undertakes a Chi-square test of independence on the 'Sex' and 'Pclass' variables. Generally such tests are conducted on samples rather than populations. The typical aim of such a test is to gain insight into whether two or more variables are independent of one another in a population of which we generally only have a sample.

When two variables are being tested, the question one seeks to answer is whether the response on one variable influences the response on another variable in the population. In the case of the impact of 'Sex' or 'Pclass' on 'Survived', if the variables were independent, the survival rate would be similar across either 'Sex' or 'Pclass'.

If the p-value resulting from such a test on a sample is very low it means that it is highly unlikely that in the population, one variable has no impact or bearing on the other. It also means that the two variables in the population likely are not independent from each other and probably have to some extent a relationship or impact between them that is apparent in the sample.

A key difference between typical Chi Square tests and what is undertaken below is that, as explained above, this data set is essentially considered a population rather than a sample. When one has access to the data for an entire population, sample related methods are not as useful or essential in conducting analysis. Furthermore, as discussed above, if this population were to be treated as a sample, given our lack of insight into how the data was collected many statistical inference techniques cannot be meaningfully used on the data.

One of the assumptions when conducting Chi Square tests is that each observation (Passenger) is independent of each other. This statement will probably not be completely true in the case of this data set given the associations between passengers.

Given these limitations and the fact that the notional larger population does not really exist if one uses this data as a sample means that one cannot draw strong conclusions from any such test. Furthermore, different social and technological trends from the time of the Titanic disaster, and the idea that past trends might not be indicative of the present or future mean that one cannot really apply the lessons or insights gained without further due diligence and research.

Even with all these limitations, a Chi Square test can at a minimum illustrate how likely or unlikely it is for two variables in a notional larger population of which this data set might be considered a sample to behave like they did in this sample if the variables were truly independent of each other.

The hypotheses for the 'Sex' and 'Survived' variables (null followed by alternative hypothesis) are:

H0: 'Sex' and 'Survived' are independent
H1: 'Sex' and 'Survived' are dependent

The hypotheses for the 'Pclass' and 'Survived' variables (null followed by alternative hypothesis) are:

H0: 'Pclass' and 'Survived' are independent
H1: 'Pclass' and 'Survived' are dependent

A 95% confidence level is being used in each case.

Thus in each of the tests below a low p-value (lower than 0.05) would mean we can reject the null hypothesis at the 95% confidence level.

In [11]:
from scipy.stats import chi2_contingency

# Grouping passengers' 'Survived' status by 'Sex' and 'Pclass' respectively 
SexSurvival = FullListDF.groupby(['Sex'])['Survived']
PclassSurvival = FullListDF.groupby(['Pclass'])['Survived']

# Creating contingency tables for 'Sex' and 'Pclass' as each relates to 'Survived'
Sex_Survived_contingency_table = SexSurvival.value_counts().unstack()
Pclass_Survived_contingency_table = PclassSurvival.value_counts().unstack()

# Deriving respective p values for each of the variables
_, Sex_p_value, _, _ = (chi2_contingency(Sex_Survived_contingency_table))
_, Pclass_p_value, _, _ = (chi2_contingency(Pclass_Survived_contingency_table))

print ('Sex and Survived -- pvalue:{}'.format(Sex_p_value))

print ('Pclass and Survived -- pvalue:{}'.format(Pclass_p_value))

Sex and Survived -- pvalue:1.1973570627755645e-58
Pclass and Survived -- pvalue:4.549251711298793e-23

Both the tests above result in p-values much lower than 0.05. The p-value is the probability of coming up with the Chi Square statistic on a test if the null hypothesis were true and in general, the lower the p-value, the higher the confidence we can have in rejecting the null hypothesis as is the case in both the tests above.

The tests confirm what we know from investigating the data that both 'Sex' and 'Pclass' influenced survival. However, as the limitations above note, one cannot draw any substantial or strong conclusions about a notional larger population or future similar disasters without further due diligence.

Conclusion¶

The analysis above shows that 'Sex', 'Pclass' (and other variables related to wealth) are the dominant factors that determined survival. In the case of 'Sex' and 'Pclass', both factors appeared to have a substantial impact as the tables and graphics above show. In addition to these, very young age also appears to be a slight factor that improved the odds of survival.

The impact of these factors seems to reflect the social conventions and economic dimensions of the period. Since we have limited information on how this information was collected and do not know if the collection might be biased in some way, it is prudent to not draw strong conclusions about the factors that led to survival.

This is particularly so in the case of the variable 'Age' since in that case there were a lot of missing values and the link or trend between age and survival is very weak as noted below.
